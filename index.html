Why two-thirds — count the ways

The simulation tests the claim. This proves it.

Three cards go in the bag. One is black on both sides, one is black on one side and white on the other, and one is white on both. McElreath's trick is to stop reasoning about cards and start counting ways the data could arise — every face that could have landed in front of you, each equally likely.

2 ways to show black
black · white
1 way to show black
white · white
0 ways to show black

A black face is staring up at you. Which faces could it be? Count them: the two-black card offers two black faces, the mixed card offers one, the white card offers none. That's three equally likely black faces you might be looking at — and on two of them, the hidden side is also black.

ways to see a black face  =  2 + 1 + 0  =  3
…of which the reverse is also black  =  2 P(reverse black | face black) = 2 / 3 ≈ 0.667

The same answer falls out of Bayes' rule, if you prefer the machinery. The black-on-both card is the only one guaranteed to show black, so once you see black it claims two-thirds of the posterior weight:

P(BB | black) = P(black | BB)·P(BB) / P(black) = (1 · ⅓) / (½) = 2⁄3 where P(black) = ⅓·1 + ⅓·½ + ⅓·0 = ½

Mind the direction. Every white face the simulation throws away is doing real work: it shows that we're asking P(reverse is black | face is black), not the other way round. Swapping the two — reading "the face is black" as if it pinned down the card — is the prosecutor's fallacy. The discard pile is the conditioning made visible.
